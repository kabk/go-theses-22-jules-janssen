All Guns No Mask

The public health domain is a place we all share. In the light of our current situation, human interdependency is becoming more tangible. While this ecosystem of health revealed itself, national lockdowns and self-isolation policies were implemented as an act of care. Yet this also triggered a counter reaction of independency and individualism. In the same breath, populist leaders like Bolsonaro promoted gun possession while abandoning COVID-19 as an innocent flu.3 Sudden manifestations of our fragile existence seem to ignite patriarchal enmity. Phenomena like Brexit and Trump shows isolationist tendencies on the global stage. But their campaigns also tap into a sentiment of individual self-containment and independency. The strong man can do it himself. These sentiments of neoliberal ableism may sound catchy to some, yet they automatically create division.4

B
Disability Justice

(Not) Surprisingly this doesn’t only stay in the medical domain. It’s treacherous how quickly the sentiment of medical ableism finds its way as a tool of othering. The disability justice framework as developed in 2005 by the Disability Justice Collective, examines ableism in relation to other forms of oppression and identity. It theorizes disability as a cultural, political, and social phenomenon, rather an individualized, medical matter attached to the body. It examines how ableism makes racism, Christian supremacy, sexism, and queer- and transphobia possible.5 While these are just a few power mechanisms, all systems of oppression are intertwined.

C
“Asylum”

It is no coincidence that the word “asylum” refers to institutionalized places for both refugees, and people suffering from a mental disorder.6 Both are poorly taken care of. Both are places far from home. Both enclose marginalized bodies. Analyzing society’s view on mental disability inevitably leads to insights concerning other labels of otherness. But perhaps their most profound similarity is their paradoxical relationship with accessibility. At the orthopedic clinic, accessibility is everything. Wheelchair ramps and elevators make sure patients can easily enter and move freely through the building. At the mental asylum, inaccessibility plays a dominant role. Often, it’s one way-in one-way out. It’s built to exclude, in order to protect “both sides" of the wall.

D
Deinstitutionalization

The mental asylum as we once knew it does not really exist anymore. As part of the world-wide deinstitutionalization process of the 20th century, the asylum made way for less isolated mental health services.7 Key-figures like Franco Basaglia and Ervin Goffman pleated for a more existential approach, countering the ‘total institution’. Complete isolation and psychopathological treatments were slowly reformed into less “penetrating” health services and provided “more freedom” to patients. Stays where shortened and admission rates reduced. This didn’t mean that the psychiatric domain suddenly became a place for freedom and equality. With the discovery of psychiatric drugs, the architectural confinement could be substituted by medicinal prescriptions. The healthcare environment became more specialized and fragmented into different narrowed care facilities. Formats such as assisted living, therapy groups, halfway houses and inpatient clinics all originated from that time.

E
Dutch Dementia

The current setting in which care is provided, differs drastically around the world. This thesis specifically explores the contemporary architectures encircling dementia care in The Netherlands. A country that is heavily capitalized, densely populated and has one of the most institutionalized elderly care systems in the world. In the Netherlands, about 115.000 people live in elderly homes.8 Roughly half of this group is afflicted by dementia. There are currently about 290.000 people experiencing dementia in the Netherlands and it this number is likely to double by 2050 because of an increasing life expectancy.9

F
Cut Off Dead End

Dementia is where the advancements in health science are confronted with a dead end. It’s a psychogeriatric condition that is not considered to be medically curable. For people with dementia this often means that the elderly home is their last resort. It requires palliative care, which means that the well-being is more important than the caregiving. This demands a huge responsibility from the care environment itself. With an already urgent shortage in caregivers and a market full of cuts, the setting surrounding people experiencing dementia is the absolute bearer of their well-being.

G
White Wash

The results are countless tayloristic rituals, captured in Excel sheets, about the right amount of light, the ideal television volume, and the average length of walks through the courtyard. But these institutional settings don’t just provide daily routine. Design interventions offer a lighting consolation for an architectural reality in which the walls are closing in. They effectively tap into a brain condition in which reality is wavering already. If we can’t be God, we can at least simulate God: murals, decors, music, and innovative simulators tend to bring residents back to the brighter days of tactile autonomy. The credibility of these escapist mediation moments depends on the pockets of the patient. This essay provides an exploration of these moments in a country that has little room for the ill.

H
Oma 'Klein Ding'

My grandma called me Wouter instead of Jules and offered me a biscuit each ten minutes. Every two weeks on a Sunday we would drive from Eindhoven to the nursing home in Bergen. We always stayed for two hours. I used to draw armless people in her room after which she would ask if I was left-handed. “Wouter is left-handed, isn’t he?” “Wouter is left-handed, isn’t he?” She passed away in October 2010. We never visited the nursing home in which she lived again. Thinking about it now, makes me remember how I used to run laps through the unit. It felt like an infinite corridor, like an endless world. The building was circular, had vinyl floors and semi-automatic push bar doors. In the middle was a little park with an artificial lake. All residents of the unit suffered from dementia.

I
The Island

Isolation derives from the Latin word “isola”, meaning island. The closed character of the memory care institution seems to be born out of modern necessity. The cortex of the human brain is humanly divided in different islands that all have different functions: forming memories, processing sound, speech production, all that jazz.2 The logic of everyday rationale is often based on similar mappings. The way in which your home is ordered, how city districts are divided and how food is categorized in supermarkets. It’s exactly in these architectural settings that things start to get lost. In the care environment art can be used as a landmark tool to improve wayfinding for its residents.

Fig 9. A room of Zorggroep Archipel, Almere, Netherlands.

J
Little Room (Room)

Cell derives from the Latin word “cellula”, meaning “small room”.3 The dementia care environment may serve as a direct analogy between the brain and the building. A healthy brain probably weighs about 1300 to 1400 kilograms. When you are in the process of depersonalizing, the environment surrounding you seems to do the same. In an Alzheimer brain plagues and tangles block the cell transport system resulting in a dysfunctional cell transport system. Nutrients and other essential supplies can no longer move through the cells. Neurons die throughout the brain, connections between networks of neurons break down and cells die.6 In the last stage of Alzheimer, a brain weights 1100 – 1200 grams.1 Daily life becomes difficult, especially in countries with a lot of roads. Trapped in the boundaries of protocol, only a tiny fragment of personal belongings makes it into the dementia unit.
